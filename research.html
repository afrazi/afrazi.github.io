Enhancing Offshore Wind Farm Efficiency Through Wake Dynamics

Wake interactions in offshore wind farms, caused by aerodynamic disturbances between turbines, represent a critical challenge to maximizing energy efficiency. These interactions reduce power output and increase mechanical stress on downstream turbines, leading to significant losses in overall performance. The understanding and mitigation of these effects are essential for advancing the sustainability and reliability of wind energy systems. In this study, a comprehensive sensitivity analysis of key operational and environmental parameters was conducted to examine their influence on wake dynamics. Factors such as turbulence intensity, yaw misalignment, and turbine operational characteristics were analyzed using advanced modeling techniques. The motivation behind this work stems from the need to address the inefficiencies that arise from wake effects in clustered turbine arrangements, which are becoming increasingly prevalent as offshore wind farms expand. The novelty of this research lies in its approach to integrating experimental data with modeling frameworks, enabling the calibration of predictive tools that reflect real-world conditions. Seasonal variations in turbulence intensity and their impact on wake recovery and power losses were a particular focus, shedding light on the temporal dynamics of offshore wind farm performance. The findings provide valuable insights into optimizing turbine layout and operational strategies to minimize energy losses caused by wake effects. By improving the accuracy of wake modeling and understanding the interplay of key parameters, this work contributes to enhancing the efficiency and reliability of offshore wind farms, aligning with global efforts to expand renewable energy capacity.

Smart Mobility Sensing for Vision-Based Passenger Origin-Destination Tracking

Understanding the movement of passengers within public transportation systems is a cornerstone of efficient transit planning, particularly in resource-constrained communities. Traditional methods for measuring origin-destination (OD) flows, such as manual driver logs, are labor-intensive and prone to inaccuracies, especially at the individual passenger level. To address these challenges, this study introduces an automated, computer vision-based sensing system for accurately detecting and tracking OD pairs. This research integrates advanced vision-based modules, including passenger detection, tracking, and re-identification, with GPS data to develop a robust and privacy-preserving approach to mobility sensing. Tailored to the environmental and demographic characteristics of resource-limited areas, the system demonstrates how affordable and scalable technologies can bridge the gap between technological limitations and the growing need for efficient transit solutions. The motivation for this study arises from the critical need to enhance transportation services in under-resourced communities where traditional smart card systems or advanced sensors are not feasible. By leveraging deep learning models and edge computing, the system achieves high accuracy in identifying passenger movements under real-world conditions, even in the presence of challenges like occlusion and pose variation. The novelty of this work lies in its systematic approach to adapting existing algorithms for localized conditions and deploying them on resource-constrained public transit systems. The results indicate that this integrated solution not only improves the precision of OD data but also provides actionable insights for optimizing routes and increasing the operational efficiency of bus systems. These advancements pave the way for broader adoption of smart mobility solutions in transit planning and management.
